Christmas in Russia

In the Christian Orthodox faith, Christmas Day in Russia commemorates the birth of Jesus Christ. Despite being outlawed during the Soviet era, Christmas is recovering popularity and religious significance in Russia.In Russia, people spend Christmas Day by having a family supper, attending a Christmas liturgy, and visiting relatives and friends. There is a 40-day Lent period preceding Christmas Day during which practicing Christians refrain from eating meat. On January 6, the first star in the night sky — a symbol of Jesus Christ's birth - marks the conclusion of the Lenten season.Orthodox Christmas symbols include a decorated fir tree, a star (such as the first star in the sky or the star on top of the Christmas tree), and baby Jesus.

Easter in Moscow

Paskha (aса) is the Russian word for Easter. The name is most likely derived from the Jewish holiday of Pesach, which commemorated the liberation of Jews from Egyptian enslavement.Because it happens on the first Sunday after the first ecclesiastical full moon, Russian Easter varies from year to year.All pre-Easter duties are meant to be completed during the last week before the celebration, often known as Holy Week.

Constitution Day in Russia

Moscow, Russia

Constitution Day (Russian: ен онститyии) is a Russian public holiday observed on December 12. It commemorates the Supreme Soviet of the RSFSR's approval of the Russian Constitution in 1993. Until 2005, Constitution Day was a day off, until the government declared it a working holiday. Constitution Day is commemorated in Moscow and St. Petersburg with ceremonial ceremonies and a fireworks show.
